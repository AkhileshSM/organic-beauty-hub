01 Foundation

Skin Anatomy

Human skin is a stratified squamous epithelium averaging 1.5–2mm in thickness (excluding subcutaneous fat). It is the body's largest organ — 1.5–2m² surface area, ~15% of body weight. Functionally it provides the primary physical and immunological barrier between the internal milieu and the external environment.

0–20μm Stratum Corneum 15–20 layers of anucleate corneocytes embedded in lamellar lipid bilayers. The primary permeability barrier. pH 4.5–5.5.
~2μm Stratum Lucidum Present only in thick skin (palms, soles). Translucent layer of dead cells.
~3μm Stratum Granulosum Lamellar bodies secreted here — ceramides, free fatty acids, cholesterol exocytosed to form SC lipid matrix.
~3μm Stratum Spinosum Desmosomes provide mechanical cohesion. Langerhans cells (immune surveillance) reside here.
~1μm Stratum Basale Proliferating keratinocyte stem cells. Melanocytes (1:10 ratio to keratinocytes). Merkel cells.
0.3–3mm Dermis Collagen (70% dry weight), elastin, glycosaminoglycans. Fibroblasts, mast cells, macrophages. Hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands.
Variable Hypodermis Adipose tissue, large vessels, nerves. Thermal insulation and mechanical cushioning.

Topical penetration routes

Cosmetic ingredients can only reach the viable epidermis if they can traverse the stratum corneum. Three routes: transcellular (through corneocytes — rare, requires very small/polar molecules), intercellular (through the lipid matrix between corneocytes — the dominant route for most lipophilic actives), and appendageal (via hair follicles and sweat ducts — a minor route but relevant for caffeine, some nanoparticles). Most actives remain in the SC — true dermal penetration is rare and often undesirable for a cosmetic (which by regulatory definition must not have systemic pharmacological effect).
02 Core function

Barrier Function

The skin barrier is a multi-component system. The mechanical metaphor — "bricks and mortar" — is the most useful: corneocytes are the bricks; the intercellular lipid matrix is the mortar. Disruption of either component compromises barrier integrity.

Stratum corneum lipid composition

  • Ceramides (~50%): 12 distinct classes in human SC. Critical for lamellar structure formation. Severely depleted in atopic dermatitis and psoriasis.
  • Cholesterol (~25%): Regulates lipid bilayer fluidity. Essential for barrier recovery speed — cholesterol synthesis inhibitors significantly delay recovery.
  • Free fatty acids (~15%): Predominantly C16–C18 saturated. Maintain the acidic pH of the SC via their acid groups. Linoleic acid deficiency causes ichthyosis-like changes.

The 1:1:1 molar ratio of ceramide:cholesterol:free fatty acid is required for lamellar body formation and secretion. Topical formulations that replicate this ratio (Physiogel, CeraVe) show significantly better barrier repair than those that don't.

Filaggrin and natural moisturising factor (NMF)

Filaggrin (FLG) is a structural protein that aggregates keratin filaments in the SC and is subsequently degraded to produce natural moisturising factor (NMF) — a complex mixture of amino acids (40%), pyrrolidone carboxylic acid (12%), lactate (12%), urea (7%), and inorganic ions that collectively maintain SC hydration and flexibility. Loss-of-function mutations in FLG are the single strongest genetic risk factor for atopic dermatitis, affecting ~10% of the European population.

Tight junctions — the second barrier

Beyond the SC lipid barrier, tight junctions (TJ) in the stratum granulosum form a second barrier against paracellular water movement. Claudin-1 and claudin-4 are the primary TJ proteins in skin. Claudin-1 expression is severely reduced in atopic dermatitis — this contributes to both increased allergen penetration and TEWL independent of SC lipid disruption.
03 Measurement

Transepidermal Water Loss

Transepidermal water loss (TEWL) is the passive diffusion of water vapour through the skin barrier. It is the gold-standard objective measurement of barrier integrity in both clinical research and cosmetic efficacy testing. Measured with a Tewameter (closed-chamber or open-chamber evaporimetry) in g/m²/h.

Factors that increase TEWL

  • Surfactant exposure (SLS, SLES) — disrupts lipid bilayer organisation
  • Low ambient humidity — increases the driving force for water evaporation
  • Over-exfoliation — removes SC layers mechanically
  • Retinoid use (initial weeks) — accelerated desquamation temporarily increases TEWL
  • Atopic dermatitis, psoriasis, ichthyosis — disease-related barrier disruption
  • Ageing — ceramide synthesis decreases ~30% per decade after age 30

How cosmetics reduce TEWL

  • Occlusives (petrolatum, dimethicone, squalane): form a semi-permeable film on SC surface, physically reducing water evaporation rate
  • Ceramide-containing formulations: replenish SC lipid matrix — reduce TEWL by restoring lamellar architecture
  • Humectants (glycerin, hyaluronic acid): retain water within SC — reduce TEWL indirectly by maintaining SC hydration gradient
  • Panthenol, Centella asiatica: stimulate keratinocyte proliferation and lamellar body secretion — active barrier repair
04 Microbiology

Skin Microbiome

The human skin hosts approximately 10¹² microorganisms across 1,000+ species. This ecosystem — the skin microbiome — is not merely commensal but functionally essential: it trains skin immunity, maintains the acid mantle, produces antimicrobial peptides, and directly competes with pathogens.

Staphylococcus epidermidis

Commensal — dominant on dry skin

Produces serine protease Esp — inhibits S. aureus biofilm formation. Ferments glycerol to produce short-chain fatty acids, lowering pH. Critically protective in atopic dermatitis against S. aureus colonisation.

Cutibacterium acnes

Commensal / conditional pathogen

Predominant organism in sebaceous follicles. Metabolises sebum triglycerides to free fatty acids — contributes to acid mantle. Specific phylotypes (IA1) linked to acne pathogenesis; others (II) associated with healthy skin. Diversity within C. acnes matters more than its total abundance.

Malassezia furfur

Commensal fungus — sebaceous sites

Obligate lipophilic yeast. Produces azelaic acid, pityriacitrin (UV-absorbing). Overgrowth linked to seborrhoeic dermatitis, dandruff, and pityriasis versicolor. Sensitive to zinc pyrithione, ketoconazole, and selenium sulphide.

Staphylococcus aureus

Opportunistic pathogen

Colonises >90% of atopic dermatitis patients vs <5% of healthy skin. Produces proteases that degrade tight junction proteins (claudin-1, occludin), lipases that deplete ceramides, and superantigens that drive Th2 immune polarisation — perpetuating the eczema cycle.

Microbiome-cosmetic interactions

  • Broad-spectrum preservatives at subinhibitory concentrations may shift microbiome composition — antimicrobial selection pressure is a concern for daily-use leave-on products
  • Surfactants (cleansers) significantly disrupt microbiome diversity — recovery takes 1–6 hours; harsh cleansing routines cumulatively alter composition
  • Postbiotics (lysates of L. rhamnosus, bacterial ferments) show promise for restoring dysbiotic skin — an emerging formulation strategy
  • pH above 6.0 in leave-on products favours S. aureus growth — acid mantle maintenance (<5.5) is an underappreciated microbiome-protective strategy
05 UV biology

Photoageing

Approximately 80% of visible facial ageing is caused by cumulative UV exposure (photoageing) rather than intrinsic chronological ageing. This distinction is clinically validated by comparing sun-exposed vs sun-protected skin on the same individual — a natural experiment.

UVC (100–280nm) — blocked by ozone UVB (280–315nm) UVA (315–400nm) Visible

UVB vs UVA mechanisms

  • UVB (280–315nm): Directly absorbed by DNA — causes cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) and 6-4 photoproducts. Primary driver of sunburn, direct DNA mutation, and squamous cell carcinoma. Blocked by glass. SPF measures UVB protection.
  • UVA (315–400nm): Penetrates deeply to the dermis. Generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) — indirect DNA damage. Degrades collagen and elastin via MMP-1, MMP-3, MMP-9 activation. Primary driver of photoageing wrinkles and pigmentation. Penetrates glass. UVA-PF (PA+++) measures UVA protection.

Collagen degradation cascade

UV activates cell surface receptors (EGF-R, TNF-R) → activates AP-1 transcription factor → upregulates MMP-1 (interstitial collagenase) expression → cleaves collagen types I, II, III. Simultaneously, UV suppresses TGF-β signalling → reduces new collagen synthesis. The result: net collagen loss with each UV exposure event, cumulative over decades.

Fitzpatrick phototype and cosmetic implications

Fitzpatrick types I–II (pale, burns easily): high photoageing risk, low melanin protection, highest skin cancer risk. Fitzpatrick types IV–VI (dark brown/black skin): significant endogenous photoprotection but higher risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) from ingredients like hydroquinone, AHAs, and strong retinoids. Ingredient selection and concentration must be adjusted by phototype.
06 Immunology

Inflammation & Skin

Cutaneous inflammation is the immune system's response to perceived threat — infection, damage, allergen, or irritant. It underlies acne, rosacea, atopic dermatitis, psoriasis, and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH). Understanding the cascade is essential for evidence-based ingredient selection.

Acute inflammation cascade

  • Trigger: Pathogen (PAMPs), damage signal (DAMPs), UV, irritant
  • Pattern recognition: Toll-like receptors (TLR-2, TLR-4) on keratinocytes and dendritic cells recognise the signal
  • NF-κB activation: Master inflammatory transcription factor — drives cytokine production (IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α)
  • Vasodilation: Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) — produced by COX-2 — causes the characteristic erythema and warmth
  • Neutrophil infiltration: IL-8 is the primary neutrophil chemoattractant — in acne, neutrophils release proteases and ROS that rupture the follicle wall
  • Resolution: IL-10, lipoxins, resolvins actively resolve inflammation — chronic inflammation occurs when resolution pathways fail

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)

Inflammation stimulates keratinocytes to produce IL-1α, endothelin-1, stem cell factor, and prostaglandin E2 — all of which activate melanocytes to increase melanin synthesis and melanosome transfer. PIH is more pronounced in Fitzpatrick types III–VI because higher baseline melanocyte activity amplifies the response. Prevention of PIH requires aggressive anti-inflammatory management alongside brightening actives.

Anti-inflammatory cosmetic actives — mechanisms

  • Niacinamide: Suppresses VEGF and IL-8; reduces melanosome transfer independently
  • Azelaic acid: Inhibits NF-κB; scavenges reactive oxygen species
  • Centella asiatica (madecassoside): Inhibits TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6 via NF-κB suppression
  • Resveratrol: SIRT-1 activator; suppresses NF-κB and AP-1 directly
  • Ferulic acid: COX-1/2 inhibitor; hydroxyl radical scavenger
  • Panthenol: Reduces prostaglandin E2; promotes resolution via keratinocyte proliferation
