01 Foundation

Emulsion Chemistry

An emulsion is a thermodynamically unstable dispersion of two immiscible liquids — typically oil and water — stabilised by surfactants (emulsifiers). Because oil and water do not mix spontaneously (ΔG mixing > 0), all emulsions eventually separate without continuous mechanical and chemical stabilisation.

There are two primary emulsion types relevant to cosmetics:

  • Oil-in-Water (O/W): Oil droplets dispersed in a continuous water phase. Feels lighter, more hydrating, better for oily/combination skin. Easier to preserve. Most lotions, serums, and fluid creams are O/W.
  • Water-in-Oil (W/O): Water droplets dispersed in a continuous oil phase. Richer, more occlusive, better for dry/barrier-compromised skin. Sunscreens and barrier creams are often W/O.

Advanced emulsion systems

  • Multiple emulsions (W/O/W or O/W/O): Useful for encapsulating incompatible actives in the inner phase. Complex to stabilise.
  • Nanoemulsions: Droplet size 20–200nm. Thermodynamically unstable but kinetically stable for years. Enhanced skin penetration, translucent appearance.
  • Microemulsions: Droplet size <20nm. Thermodynamically stable (ΔG < 0). Form spontaneously. Require high surfactant concentrations (15–25%).
  • Pickering emulsions: Stabilised by solid particles (silica, clay, starch) rather than molecular surfactants. Increasingly popular for clean beauty formulations.

Ostwald Ripening

The primary degradation pathway for nanoemulsions. Smaller droplets dissolve and redeposit on larger ones over time — driven by the Laplace pressure difference between droplets of different sizes. Mitigated by using oils with very low water solubility (triglycerides > mineral oil) or adding a ripening inhibitor (e.g. hexadecane at 5–10% of oil phase).
02 Emulsifier selection

HLB Theory

The Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance (HLB) is a numerical scale (0–20) quantifying the relative affinity of an emulsifier for water vs oil phases. Developed by William Griffin at Atlas Chemical in 1949, it remains the primary tool for rational emulsifier selection.

03 Manufacturing

Phase Behaviour

Most emulsions are manufactured using a three-phase approach. Each phase is prepared separately at temperature, then combined in a controlled sequence to achieve droplet size, homogeneity, and stability.

Water Phase

Heat to 70–80°C

Deionised water · Humectants (glycerin, glycols) · Water-soluble actives · Hydrophilic polymers (carbomer, HEC) · EDTA chelating agent

Oil Phase

Heat to 70–80°C

Emollients (esters, oils) · Emulsifiers · Waxes and butters · Oil-soluble actives · Vitamins E, A

Cool-down Phase

Add below 40°C

Heat-sensitive actives (vitamin C, retinol, peptides) · Fragrance · Preservatives · pH adjusters · Silicones

Critical manufacturing rule

Always add the water phase to the oil phase (for W/O) or oil phase to the water phase (for O/W) slowly and with continuous agitation. Reversed addition order or incorrect temperature matching causes phase inversion or catastrophic demulsification.
04 Formulation parameter

pH Optimisation

pH is arguably the single most important formulation variable. It affects active ingredient efficacy, preservative system activity, skin compatibility, gelling agent performance, and long-term stability simultaneously.

Skin-compatible pH range

Healthy skin surface pH is 4.5–5.5 (the "acid mantle"). Formulations significantly outside this range disrupt the acid mantle, impair serine protease activity, and reduce ceramide synthesis enzyme activity. For leave-on products, targeting pH 4.5–5.5 is both skin-compatible and optimal for most actives.

L-Ascorbic Acid2.5–3.5Protonated form required for skin penetration; oxidises rapidly above pH 4

pH adjusters in formulation

  • Citric acid / lactic acid: Lower pH — compatible with skin, buffering capacity, also mild humectants
  • Sodium hydroxide (NaOH): Raise pH — use dilute solution (10–18% w/w); neutralises carbomers to activate gelling
  • Triethanolamine (TEA): Raise pH — traditional carbomer neutraliser, but flagged for nitrosamine formation risk
  • Aminomethyl propanol (AMP): Raise pH — preferred modern alternative to TEA; lower nitrosamine risk
05 Microbiology

Preservation

Preservation is the most technically demanding aspect of cosmetic formulation. An inadequately preserved product can become contaminated with Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus aureus, Candida albicans, or Aspergillus — causing serious consumer harm. EU Regulation 1223/2009 and ISO 11930 mandate efficacy testing.

Preservative challenge test (PCT) — ISO 11930:2019

The PCT inoculates a product with five standardised microorganisms at a defined concentration, incubates for 28 days, and measures log reduction at days 2, 7, 14, and 28. Products must achieve Category A (stringent) or Category B (acceptable) criteria.

Modern preservative systems

  • Phenoxyethanol (0.8–1%) + Ethylhexylglycerin (0.2%): Industry standard. Broad spectrum. EU max 1% total.
  • Sodium benzoate (0.5%) + Potassium sorbate (0.5%): Natural-origin. pH-dependent — only effective below pH 5.5. Popular in "clean" formulations.
  • Caprylyl glycol + Hexanediol: Multifunctional — humectant + mild antimicrobial. Requires boosters for full spectrum coverage.
  • Benzyl alcohol (0.5–1%) + Dehydroacetic acid (0.2%): "Natural" alternative. Limited evidence base vs phenoxyethanol systems.

The preservation paradox

The highest-risk products are water-based formulations sold as "preservative-free." Without an effective preservative system, microbial contamination during consumer use is a serious safety risk. "Free-from" marketing must never compromise preservation efficacy.
06 Quality assurance

Stability Testing

Stability testing confirms a formulation maintains its physical, chemical, and microbiological integrity over its intended shelf life. EU Regulation 1223/2009 requires stability data to support a product's Period After Opening (PAO) or minimum durability date.

Standard stability protocol

  • Real-time stability: 25°C / 60% RH for 24 months (full shelf-life confirmation)
  • Accelerated stability: 40°C / 75% RH for 6 months (predictive — correlates approximately to 2 years at 25°C)
  • Freeze-thaw cycling: 5 cycles between −10°C and +25°C — tests emulsion robustness
  • Photo-stability: UV/VIS exposure per ICH Q1B — critical for vitamin C, retinol, and coloured formulations
  • Thermal shock: 4°C ↔ 45°C rapid cycling — stress testing of emulsifier system

Parameters measured at each timepoint

  • Appearance (phase separation, sedimentation, colour change)
  • pH (drift indicates degradation reactions)
  • Viscosity (rheology shift indicates emulsifier breakdown)
  • Droplet size distribution (D50, D90 via laser diffraction)
  • Active ingredient assay (HPLC — confirms potency retention)
  • Microbial limits (per ISO 17516)
  • Odour (organoleptic)
07 Rheology

Viscosity & Rheology

Rheology — the study of flow and deformation — determines how a product feels, spreads, and deposits on skin. Most cosmetic formulations are non-Newtonian: their viscosity changes under shear stress (pumping, rubbing, spreading).

Key rheological behaviours

  • Shear-thinning (pseudoplastic): Viscosity decreases under shear. Ideal for pump dispensers — flows easily when pumped, thickens on skin. Most creams and lotions. Examples: carbomer gels, xanthan gum systems.
  • Thixotropy: Time-dependent shear thinning — returns to original viscosity after rest. Luxury creams often exploit this for a "rich but absorbing" feel.
  • Yield stress fluids: Don't flow until a minimum stress is exceeded. Useful for rinse-off products — stays where applied until rinsed. Carbopol ETD 2020 systems.

Common thickeners and their mechanisms

  • Carbomers (Carbopol): Polyacrylic acid. pH-activated swelling — neutralise to pH 6–7 for maximum viscosity. Highly efficient: 0.3–1% achieves gel consistency.
  • Xanthan gum: Microbial polysaccharide. Excellent salt tolerance. Synergistic with guar gum. Typical use 0.3–0.8%.
  • Hydroxypropyl methylcellulose (HPMC): Semi-synthetic cellulose ether. Thermal gelation (gels on heating, liquefies on cooling). Good clarity.
  • Cetyl/cetearyl alcohol: Fatty alcohols thicken O/W emulsions via lamellar liquid crystal formation — contributes simultaneously to emulsion stability and skin feel.
